Compliance and food safety

Get it right from day one.
Before it becomes a problem.

Food safety compliance is not optional and it is not one size fits all. Whether you are registering your first food business or preparing for a buyer audit, the paperwork needs to reflect how your operation actually works. Not how someone else's does.

Why it matters

Most small producers are one inspection away from a serious problem.

Environmental health officers visit unannounced. Buyers run due diligence before they place an order. Retailers ask for documentation before they will stock you. If your food safety management system exists only in your head, or as a folder of documents you downloaded and never properly adapted, you are exposed.

Getting compliant does not have to be expensive, slow or complicated. Built correctly from the start, a food safety management system runs in the background without demanding constant attention. The hard work happens once. After that, compliance becomes part of how you operate rather than a separate job on top of it.

The regulations

What the law actually requires.

These are the key pieces of legislation that apply to most small food and drink businesses in the UK.

Food Safety Act 1990

The foundation of UK food law. It makes it a criminal offence to sell food that is unsafe, unfit or not what it claims to be. It establishes the due diligence defence, meaning that if something goes wrong, you need documentation proving you took all reasonable precautions.

Regulation (EC) No 852/2004

Retained in UK law post-Brexit. Requires all food businesses to implement a food safety management system based on HACCP principles. Identifying hazards, putting controls in place, monitoring those controls and keeping records. For most small producers this is the single most important document to get right.

Food Information Regulations 2014

Governs what must appear on labels and how allergens must be declared. The 14 major allergens must be clearly highlighted. For producers with complex or botanical recipes, allergen management is one of the highest risk areas in the business.

Food Business Registration

Any food business must register with its local authority at least 28 days before starting to trade. Free, mandatory and often missed by startups focused on everything else. Registration triggers the environmental health inspection cycle and puts you on the right side of the law from day one.

Food Standards Act 1999 and local authority enforcement

Local authorities have the power to inspect, issue improvement notices, suspend operations and prosecute. The Food Hygiene Rating Scheme is increasingly used by buyers and consumers as a quick indicator of standards. A low rating is hard to recover from commercially.

SALSA standard

The highest bar in the industry.
Applied to every piece of work.

Mucked* uses the SALSA Food and Drink Production standard as its baseline for every engagement. Not because every client needs formal SALSA certification, but because building to that standard means everything is done properly from the start and everything that matters is covered.

What SALSA is

The standard written specifically for small food and drink producers.

SALSA stands for Safe and Local Supplier Approval. It is a food safety certification scheme designed specifically for small and micro food and drink producers in the UK, created because large food safety standards like BRC and FSSC were built for factories, not small producers making things in small batches.

SALSA covers twelve areas of food safety management. Every document, procedure and record required by SALSA also satisfies the corresponding legal requirement. There is no duplication. Build once to the highest standard and everything is covered.

Not every festival is your festival

The right event makes the difference. The wrong one just costs money.

One of the most valuable things Mucked* can help with before you book a single pitch is working out which events actually make sense for your food. Getting the match right between what you sell and who is in that field is the difference between a great weekend and an expensive lesson.

There is no point taking heavy comfort food to a large electronic music festival with a young crowd and a lineup full of DJ acts. A significant portion of that crowd will be on class A drugs and they are not hungry. Your takings will reflect that. The same food at a folk festival, a food and drink event or a family weekend will perform completely differently. Knowing this before you pay the pitch fee matters.

Good matches

Folk, food and drink, family, artisan, literary, community and cultural festivals. Audiences engaged with food, willing to spend, actively looking for something interesting. Smaller events with the right demographic will often outperform large poorly matched ones.

Poor matches

Events where the crowd is not focused on food. Pitches that cost more than they return. Always run the numbers before committing.
