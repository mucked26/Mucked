SALSA and audits

The questions everyone asks before their first audit.

Do I actually need SALSA certification?

Not always, but building to SALSA standard is almost always worth doing regardless. SALSA certification is a commercial requirement if you want to supply certain retailers, wholesalers and food service buyers. Without it, some doors simply will not open. But even if your immediate buyers do not require the certificate, the SALSA standard is the most rigorous framework designed specifically for small producers. Building to it means your compliance is genuinely solid rather than just about passing the minimum legal bar. Most clients find that by the time they need the certificate, the hard work is already done.

How long does it take to get SALSA ready?

For a business starting from scratch with no documentation in place, realistically three to six months to have everything built, embedded and genuinely audit-ready. The documentation itself can be produced faster than that, but the audit requires evidence that your systems are actually being used, not just written down. Auditors want to see completed records, review meetings that have happened, and staff who understand the procedures. Rushing the timeline tends to produce documentation that looks right on paper but falls apart when an auditor starts asking questions.

Can I pass SALSA without hiring a consultant?

Technically yes. The SALSA guidance documents are publicly available and some businesses do prepare themselves. In practice, most first attempts without support either take much longer than expected or fail at the first audit. The most common issue is not that the documents are wrong, it is that the systems are not properly embedded in how the business actually operates. An auditor will spot quickly if a HACCP plan was written to pass an audit rather than written to reflect real practice. If you have strong operational experience and plenty of time, self-preparation is possible. If your time is better spent running the business, the cost of getting it done properly is usually recovered quickly in avoided re-audit fees and lost commercial opportunities.

What are the most common reasons businesses fail a SALSA audit?

The most common failure points are incomplete traceability records, allergen management that looks correct on paper but has gaps in practice, HACCP plans that reference equipment or processes that do not match the actual operation, and supplier approval lists that have not been maintained. A close second is documentation that exists but nobody in the team actually uses or understands. An auditor will interview staff as well as review documents. If your team cannot explain why a procedure exists or what they do when something goes wrong, that is a finding regardless of how good the paperwork looks.

What is the difference between SALSA and BRCGS?

SALSA was designed specifically for small and micro food and drink producers. BRCGS was designed for large food manufacturing operations and is significantly more demanding in terms of infrastructure, resource and ongoing audit frequency. If you are a small producer, SALSA is almost certainly the right standard to target. BRCGS becomes relevant if you are supplying certain major retailers who specifically require it, or if your operation has grown to a scale where SALSA is no longer the appropriate framework. For most small producers, BRCGS is disproportionate to their size and a SALSA certificate will satisfy the commercial requirements they actually face.

How much paperwork do I really need?

More than most small businesses have in place when they first seek help, but less than most people fear when they start looking at the requirements. The paperwork itself is not the point. The point is that your food safety management system is documented, followed and reviewed. A well-built system for a small producer typically covers twelve areas and produces maybe twenty to thirty core documents plus ongoing records. The difference between a system that works and one that is a paperwork burden is whether it was built around how your business actually operates, or whether it was written to satisfy a checklist.

Can I use Notion or digital systems for SALSA documentation?

Yes, and for most small businesses a well-built digital system is significantly better than paper or spreadsheets. SALSA does not specify how records must be kept, only that they must be kept, accessible and auditable. A Notion database with version-controlled documents, completed record templates and a clear audit trail satisfies this requirement and is often easier to demonstrate to an auditor than a folder of printed forms. The key is that the system is genuinely being used, records are dated and completed, and there is a clear document control procedure showing version history.

HACCP and food safety

What the law actually requires and what it means in practice.

Do I legally need a HACCP plan?

Yes. Regulation (EC) No 852/2004, retained in UK law after Brexit, requires all food businesses to implement a food safety management system based on HACCP principles. This applies to every food business regardless of size, from a one-person kitchen operation to a large manufacturer. There is some flexibility in how small businesses document their HACCP approach, but the underlying requirement to identify hazards, put controls in place and keep records is not optional. Environmental health officers will ask to see evidence of your HACCP approach and the records it generates.

Can I just download a HACCP template online?

You can, but it will not do what you need it to do. A HACCP plan must reflect your specific products, your specific equipment, your specific premises and your specific processes. A template downloaded from the internet reflects someone else's operation. When an environmental health officer or SALSA auditor reviews your HACCP plan, they will ask detailed questions about the critical control points it identifies and why. If your plan references a blast chiller you do not have, or does not cover a hazard specific to your process, that is an immediate problem. Templates are a starting point for understanding the structure, not a substitute for a plan built around your actual operation.

How often should HACCP documents be reviewed?

Your HACCP plan should be reviewed whenever something significant changes in your operation, and formally at least once a year as a minimum. Significant changes include introducing a new product, changing a supplier, modifying a process, adding new equipment, changing premises or adding allergens to your product range. Many small businesses have a HACCP plan that was written when they started and has never been touched since. If your operation has evolved and your HACCP plan has not, the plan is not reflecting the actual risks in your business.

What is the difference between HACCP and a food safety management system?

HACCP is the hazard analysis methodology at the core of a food safety management system. It is the process of identifying what could go wrong with food safety in your operation and putting controls in place to prevent it. A food safety management system is the broader framework that includes your HACCP plan plus all the supporting procedures, records, training, cleaning, pest control, allergen management and supplier control that sit around it. Think of HACCP as the engine and the food safety management system as the whole vehicle.

Do I need separate HACCP plans for different products?

Not necessarily separate plans, but your HACCP analysis needs to cover all the products you produce and the hazards associated with each. If your products are broadly similar in terms of ingredients and process, one HACCP plan with product-specific sections is usually sufficient. If you produce very different product types with fundamentally different processes and hazard profiles, separate plans may make more sense. The key question is whether a single document can clearly demonstrate that every product you make has been properly risk assessed. If it cannot, it needs to be expanded or split.

What records am I legally required to keep?

As a minimum, you are required to keep records demonstrating that your food safety management system is being followed. In practice this means temperature records, cleaning records, supplier delivery records, training records, pest control records and evidence of HACCP review. The Food Safety Act 1990 requires you to be able to demonstrate due diligence, meaning that if something goes wrong, you can show you took all reasonable precautions. Businesses that cannot produce records when asked face the uncomfortable position of having no defence. For distilleries and other licensed producers, HMRC excise records are an additional legal requirement on top of food safety documentation.

What food safety paperwork should I have from day one?

From the moment you start trading you should have: food business registration with your local authority, a HACCP plan covering your products and processes, a cleaning schedule, allergen information for all products, supplier records for your key ingredients, and basic temperature monitoring records if you are storing chilled or frozen food. This is the minimum. It is not a lot and it does not need to be complicated, but businesses that start without it tend to find themselves scrambling to produce documentation retrospectively when a buyer asks or an inspection happens.

Environmental health and compliance

What to expect and how to be ready.

What happens during an environmental health inspection?

An environmental health officer will visit your premises, usually unannounced, and assess three things: how hygienically you handle food, your physical condition and cleanliness, and your food safety management system and record keeping. They will look at your premises, your equipment, your storage, your temperature controls and your staff practices. They will also ask to see your documentation and may ask staff members questions directly. The inspection results in a food hygiene rating from zero to five. Most of what gets a business a lower rating is documentation that is absent, incomplete or clearly not being used in practice.

How do I prepare for my first inspection?

Have your documentation in order before you start trading, not after your first inspection. Make sure your HACCP plan is written, your cleaning schedule is being followed and the records are completed, your temperature monitoring is in place and recorded, your allergen information is accurate and accessible, and your staff know the basics of your food safety procedures. The biggest preparation mistake is treating the inspection as a one-off event to prepare for rather than building systems that mean you are always ready. Inspections happen unannounced. The only reliable preparation is operating correctly every day.

What food hygiene rating can I realistically expect as a new business?

A new business with proper documentation, clean premises and good food hygiene practice should expect a four or five. The rating reflects the state of your operation on the day of the inspection. Businesses that get a three or below on a first inspection almost always have gaps in their documentation rather than problems with physical hygiene. A missing HACCP plan, incomplete records or allergen information that is not properly managed will bring a rating down regardless of how clean the kitchen is. Five is achievable from day one if the systems are in place.

What documents will an Environmental Health Officer ask to see?

Typically: your HACCP plan and associated records, your cleaning schedule and cleaning records, temperature monitoring records for fridges, freezers and cooking, allergen information for your products, supplier records and delivery notes, staff training records, and pest control records. Some officers will also ask for your food business registration confirmation, your product specifications and your traceability records showing how you would trace an ingredient from supplier to customer. Having these in a clearly organised folder or digital system means an inspection feels like a straightforward conversation rather than a scramble.

What happens if I get a poor food hygiene rating?

Commercially it can be damaging. The rating is published online and many buyers, retailers and consumers check it before making decisions. A rating below three can lose you accounts and make it significantly harder to win new ones. Practically, a poor rating triggers a requirement to address the findings, and you may face a re-inspection. The good news is that ratings can be improved, and many businesses that started with a low rating have recovered to a five with the right systems in place. The process is structured: understand exactly what the officer found, fix it properly, and request a re-inspection. Patching problems superficially before a re-inspection without fixing the underlying system usually results in the same issues surfacing again.

How quickly can problems be fixed after an inspection?

Documentation problems can often be addressed within days to weeks. Physical premises issues depend on the nature of the work required. The timeline for a re-inspection is typically a minimum of three months from the original visit, though this varies by local authority. The more important question is whether the fix is genuine. Writing a HACCP plan in a hurry after an inspection without properly understanding your hazards and controls produces documentation that will fail again. The businesses that recover fastest and stay at a five rating are the ones that treat the inspection findings as an opportunity to build the right systems rather than the minimum required to pass.

Starting a food or drink business

What you need before you start trading.

What do I need before I can legally sell food?

You must register your food business with your local authority at least 28 days before you start trading. This is free and mandatory. Beyond registration, you need a food safety management system in place before you start, which means a HACCP plan, cleaning procedures, allergen management and basic record keeping. If your products carry allergen information, it must be accurate. If you are producing alcohol, you need HMRC registration before your first duty-paying production. None of this needs to be complicated at the start, but it needs to exist. The businesses that get into difficulty are the ones that start trading and think they will sort the paperwork later. Later tends to arrive in the form of an inspection or a buyer asking for documentation you do not have.

Do I need to register my food business before trading?

Yes. Food business registration with your local authority is a legal requirement under the Food Standards Act 1999. You must register at least 28 days before you start trading. It is free, it takes about ten minutes online, and you cannot be refused. Many startups miss this because they are focused on everything else involved in launching and assume it will sort itself out. It will not sort itself out. An unregistered food business is operating illegally and has no food hygiene rating, which creates immediate problems with any buyer or retailer who asks to see it.

What records should I start keeping immediately?

From day one: temperature records for any chilled or frozen storage, delivery records for incoming ingredients, cleaning records, and any records your HACCP plan requires you to keep. If you are a distillery or licensed producer, HMRC duty records from your first production. The instinct to sort records out later is understandable when you are trying to do everything at once, but incomplete records from the early days of a business are almost impossible to reconstruct. Start simple, be consistent, and build from there.

When should I think about SALSA certification?

Earlier than most people do. The common pattern is to start thinking about SALSA when a buyer asks for it, at which point there is pressure to get certified quickly. A better approach is to build to SALSA standard from the beginning, even if formal certification is not an immediate commercial requirement. The cost of building your systems to SALSA standard at the start is not much more than building them to a lower standard, and the value of having certification available when a buyer asks for it, rather than scrambling to get it, is significant. If you are building systems from scratch, build them right the first time.

What systems should I put in place before I grow?

Before you add staff, add products or add sites, you need your core systems documented and working. That means a food safety management system that reflects your actual operation, a traceability system that can scale, a production recording process that does not rely on memory or whiteboard notes, and a document control system so that when procedures change, old versions do not keep circulating. The businesses that grow smoothly are the ones that built proper operational infrastructure before they needed it. The businesses that struggle are the ones trying to document what they do at the same time as training new staff, preparing for an audit and launching a new product.

Festival and event operations

What you need to trade at festivals and what catches people out.

What paperwork do I need to trade at festivals?

Most festival organisers will require as a minimum: your food business registration, your food hygiene rating certificate, your public liability insurance, a food safety management system or HACCP summary, and allergen information for your products. Some larger events will require additional documentation including a risk assessment, gas safety certificates if you are using LPG, electrical certificates and a fire risk assessment for your unit. The documentation requirements vary by organiser and event size. The safest approach is to have all of it in order before your first season, not to wait until an organiser asks for something specific.

What food safety requirements do festival organisers usually ask for?

At a minimum, proof of food business registration and a current food hygiene rating. Most serious events will also want to see your food safety management documentation, allergen information and evidence that your staff have food hygiene training. Some events have their own compliance forms that you fill in as part of the application process. The requirements have tightened significantly over the last ten years. An event that accepted a basic hygiene certificate five years ago may now require a full documentation pack before they will confirm your pitch. Getting a rejection after you have already planned your season around an event is an expensive lesson.

How do I manage allergens at events?

This is one of the highest risk areas for festival caterers and one of the most commonly mishandled. Your allergen information must be accurate, clearly displayed and kept up to date if your recipe or suppliers change. Cross-contamination controls need to be practical for your setup, which is more challenging in a temporary kitchen than in a fixed premises. Staff need to understand your allergens and know what to say when a customer asks. The most dangerous scenario is a member of staff giving incorrect allergen information because they are not sure and do not want to disappoint a customer. A written allergen policy, clear labelling and regular team briefings are the minimum. At a busy festival with a queue building, allergen management has to be automatic rather than something people have to think about under pressure.

What are the biggest mistakes first-time festival traders make?

Underestimating the paperwork requirements and leaving it until the week before the event. Choosing the wrong events, specifically large music festivals with demographics that are not aligned with the food being sold. Underestimating costs, particularly pitch fees, equipment hire, staffing and food waste. Over-preparing on volume and running out of a key ingredient, or under-preparing and selling out on day one without enough product for day two. Not having systems that work in a field, so what works in a fixed kitchen becomes chaotic in a temporary setup. And not having anyone to call when something goes wrong on site. The common thread is a lack of operational planning that turns manageable problems into expensive ones.

What documents should I keep on-site at events?

Your food business registration confirmation, your food hygiene rating certificate, your allergen information, your food safety management summary or HACCP overview, your temperature monitoring records for that day, and your cleaning records. If you are using gas, your gas safety certificate. If your event runs over multiple days, daily records for each day. An environmental health officer can and does inspect at festivals and events, and a temporary setup is treated as a food business in exactly the same way as a permanent kitchen. Having everything in a clearly labelled folder in the unit is the simplest solution.

How do I prepare for an inspection during an event?

The honest answer is that you prepare by operating correctly every day, not by doing anything differently when an officer appears. If your temperature monitoring is up to date, your allergen information is accurate, your food is stored and handled correctly and your records are complete, an inspection during an event is a ten-minute conversation rather than a problem. The businesses that struggle during on-site inspections are the ones that cut corners under the pressure of a busy service. An officer who sees food stored incorrectly, temperatures not being checked or staff handling food with poor hygiene practice will not be satisfied by a folder of documents showing what should be happening.

How much does SALSA certification cost?
The honest answer.

SALSA certification involves several different costs and most businesses do not account for all of them upfront. This page breaks down what you will actually spend.

The SALSA fees

What SALSA itself charges.

SALSA certification involves a membership fee and an audit fee. Membership is currently around £400 per year. The initial audit fee varies depending on the size and complexity of your operation but is typically in the range of £500 to £800 for a small producer. These are the fees you pay directly to SALSA. They are not the largest part of the cost.

If you fail the initial audit and need a re-audit, you pay the audit fee again. This is one of the reasons that getting proper preparation in place before the audit matters commercially, not just operationally.

The preparation cost

The part most businesses underestimate.

The SALSA fee is not the biggest cost. The biggest cost is getting your operation to the standard required. SALSA covers twelve compliance areas. For a business starting from scratch with no documentation in place, building everything to SALSA standard involves significant time and resource.

If you do it yourself, the cost is primarily your time. Realistically, a founder with no compliance background building to SALSA standard from scratch should expect to spend several months and a significant number of hours on documentation, review and embedding procedures into daily operations. That time has a cost even if it is not an invoice.

Can I write my own HACCP plan?
Yes. But here is what most people get wrong.

Writing your own HACCP plan is legally permitted and some businesses do it well. Most do not, and the failures tend to follow the same patterns. This page explains what a HACCP plan actually needs to do and where self-written ones usually fall short.

What a HACCP plan actually is

Not a document. A system.

The most common misunderstanding about HACCP plans is treating them as a document to produce rather than a system to build. A HACCP plan is a documented analysis of every hazard in your production process, the controls you have put in place to manage each one, the monitoring procedures that confirm those controls are working, and the corrective actions you take when something goes wrong. It is a living document that reflects how your operation actually works, not a description of how a generic food business operates.

The Food Standards Agency publishes guidance on HACCP that is freely available and genuinely useful for understanding the framework. Reading it before attempting to write your own plan is strongly recommended.

Where self-written plans go wrong

The four most common failure points.

Hazard identification is incomplete

Most self-written HACCP plans identify the obvious hazards and miss the less obvious ones. Allergen cross-contamination, chemical contamination from cleaning agents, physical hazards from equipment, and microbiological hazards specific to your ingredient combinations all need to be assessed. Missing a hazard is not just a documentation problem. It is a genuine food safety risk.

Critical control points are not correctly identified

A critical control point is a step in your process where a control measure can be applied to prevent, eliminate or reduce a food safety hazard to an acceptable level. Identifying too many CCPs makes the system unmanageable. Identifying too few means genuine risks are not being controlled. Getting this right requires a genuine understanding of your process and the hazards associated with it.

The plan does not reflect the actual operation

A HACCP plan that references a walk-in chiller you do not have, a cooking process you do not use or equipment that was replaced two years ago is not just technically wrong. It is actively misleading to an environmental health officer or auditor, and it provides no protection under the due diligence defence if something goes wrong.

It is written once and never reviewed

A HACCP plan must be reviewed whenever anything significant changes in your operation. New products, new equipment, new suppliers, new staff, new premises. A plan written on day one that has never been touched is almost certainly out of date and will not withstand scrutiny.

When writing your own makes sense

And when it probably does not.

If you have a genuine understanding of food safety hazards relevant to your products, the time to invest in doing it properly, and a straightforward production process, writing your own HACCP plan is achievable. The FSA guidance and the Safer Food Better Business toolkit are both useful starting points.

If your products are complex, your ingredient supply chain involves allergen risk, your process involves multiple steps with genuine hazard points, or your ambition includes SALSA certification or retail supply, getting it done properly by someone who understands food production is likely to cost less in the long run than rebuilding an inadequate plan when a buyer or auditor finds the gaps.

What happens during an environmental health inspection?
What to expect and how to be ready.

Environmental health inspections happen unannounced. The only reliable preparation is operating correctly every day. This page explains what an officer actually looks at, what gets businesses into trouble, and what a good inspection looks like.

What an officer actually assesses

Three areas. All of them matter.

Environmental health officers assess food businesses against three broad areas. How hygienically food is handled and prepared. The physical condition and cleanliness of the premises and equipment. And the food safety management system, which means the documentation, records and procedures in place to ensure food safety is being managed properly.

The food hygiene rating you receive reflects the worst of these three areas, not the average. A spotlessly clean kitchen with no documentation will not receive a five. A business with excellent documentation but poor temperature control will not receive a five. All three need to be in good order.

What officers look at in detail

The specifics that determine your rating.

Temperature controls

Fridge and freezer temperatures, cooking temperatures, chilling procedures and hot holding. The officer will check the actual temperatures in your equipment and ask to see your temperature monitoring records. Records that exist but are not being completed consistently are a red flag.

Cleaning and hygiene

The physical condition of surfaces, equipment and food contact materials. Your cleaning schedule and the records showing it is being followed. The condition of handwashing facilities. Cross-contamination controls between raw and ready-to-eat food.

Documentation

Your HACCP plan, cleaning records, temperature logs, allergen information, supplier records, training records and pest control records. The officer will ask to see these and may ask staff members questions about them. Documents that exist but nobody uses or understands are worse than useful in an inspection.

Allergen management

How allergens are identified, controlled and communicated to customers. This area has become significantly more scrutinised following high-profile allergen incidents in the UK food industry. Inaccurate allergen information is treated as a serious finding.

What gets businesses into trouble

The most common reasons for a poor rating.

The most common reason for a rating below four is documentation that is absent, incomplete or clearly not being used. A HACCP plan that was written two years ago and never reviewed. Temperature records with blank entries. Cleaning records that are identical every day in a way that suggests they are being filled in retrospectively rather than in real time. Training records that do not exist.

The second most common reason is physical premises issues that have been allowed to deteriorate. Damaged surfaces that cannot be cleaned effectively, inadequate handwashing facilities, poor pest control or food stored in a way that creates cross-contamination risk.

Neither of these is difficult to address if you know what is expected. The businesses that receive poor ratings are almost always the ones that have not prioritised compliance rather than the ones that have tried and failed.

After an inspection

What happens if you get a poor rating.

A poor food hygiene rating is published online and is increasingly checked by buyers, retailers and consumers. A rating below three can lose you accounts and make winning new ones significantly harder. The rating can be improved, but there is typically a minimum of three months before a re-inspection can be requested, and the local authority controls the timeline.

Improvement notices have legal force and deadlines. If an officer issues an improvement notice, the required actions must be completed within the specified timeframe. Getting the right documentation and systems in place quickly and correctly is exactly the kind of situation Mucked* is built for.

What food safety records am I legally required to keep?
The minimum. And why you should do more than the minimum.

The law specifies what food businesses must document and record. This page covers what is legally required, what is expected in practice, and what records will protect your business if something goes wrong.

The legal requirements

What the law actually says.

Regulation (EC) No 852/2004, retained in UK law after Brexit, requires food businesses to implement and maintain a food safety management system based on HACCP principles, keep records demonstrating that the system is being followed, and retain those records for a period appropriate to the shelf life of the products.

The Food Safety Act 1990 establishes the due diligence defence. If something goes wrong with food safety in your business, the defence requires you to demonstrate that you took all reasonable precautions and exercised all due diligence. Without records, there is no defence. This is the practical reason why record keeping matters beyond regulatory compliance.

Records you are required to keep

The minimum that an environmental health officer will expect to see.

Temperature monitoring records

Fridge and freezer temperature logs. Cooking temperature records where cooking is part of your process. Chilling records where food is cooled after cooking. These need to be completed in real time, not retrospectively.

Cleaning records

A cleaning schedule showing what is cleaned, how often and with what. Records confirming the schedule is being followed. Signed and dated by the person who carried out the cleaning.

Supplier and delivery records

Records of approved suppliers, delivery notes and incoming goods checks. These form the first link in your traceability chain and are required to demonstrate that your raw materials are sourced from reputable, approved suppliers.

Training records

Records showing that all food handlers have received appropriate food hygiene training. The level of training required depends on the role, but every person handling food must have evidence of training in place.

Pest control records

Evidence of a pest management programme, whether contracted out or managed internally. Inspection reports, treatment records and any findings and actions taken.

HACCP review records

Records showing that your HACCP plan is reviewed regularly and whenever significant changes occur. Review meetings should be documented with findings and any updates recorded.

For distilleries and licensed producers

Additional legal record keeping requirements.

If you produce dutiable alcohol, HMRC requires you to keep excise records covering production volumes, alcohol strength measurements, bulk stock accounts, movements of goods under duty suspension, and dispatch records. These records must be retained for a minimum of six years. HMRC has the right to inspect these records and discrepancies can result in duty assessments and penalties.

Excise record keeping requirements are in addition to food safety documentation, not instead of it. A distillery must maintain both sets of records to the required standard.

Beyond the minimum

Why the businesses that do more than the minimum are better protected.

The legal minimum is the floor, not the standard to aim for. Businesses that maintain thorough records are better protected in three ways. They can demonstrate due diligence if a food safety incident occurs. They can respond quickly to buyer traceability requests and auditor questions. And they can identify problems in their operation before those problems cause harm or trigger enforcement action.

The businesses that get into serious difficulty are rarely the ones that tried to maintain good records and fell short. They are almost always the ones that decided records were a bureaucratic burden rather than operational protection and stopped keeping them properly.

What SALSA is

The food safety standard designed specifically for small producers.

SALSA stands for Safe and Local Supplier Approval. It is a food safety certification scheme created specifically for small and micro food and drink producers in the UK. It was developed because the large food safety standards, BRC and FSSC 22000, were built for industrial food manufacturers and were wholly disproportionate for small producers making food in small batches.

SALSA is managed by the SALSA organisation and supported by the major UK food industry trade bodies. Certification is recognised by retailers, wholesalers, food service buyers and hospitality operators across the UK as a credible, independently audited standard that small producers can realistically achieve and maintain.

The standard covers twelve areas of food safety management. Every document, procedure and record required by SALSA also satisfies the corresponding UK legal requirement. Build once to SALSA standard and your legal compliance is covered with no duplication.

The twelve compliance areas

What SALSA actually covers.

01

Food safety management

HACCP plan, review procedure, food safety culture policy and internal audit records. The core of the standard and the area most businesses need to build from scratch.

02

Allergen management

Allergen register, risk assessment, cross-contamination controls and label verification. Critical for producers with complex or botanical recipes, multiple product lines or shared equipment.

03

Supplier and raw material control

Approved supplier list, input specifications, incoming goods inspection and food fraud risk assessment. Demonstrates that you know where your ingredients come from and have verified their suitability.

04

Premises and equipment

Site floor plan, glass register, maintenance programme and foreign body controls. Your premises and equipment must be fit for purpose and properly maintained.

05

Cleaning and hygiene

Cleaning schedules, chemical COSHH records, cleaning verification and Listeria monitoring where applicable. Evidence that your cleaning programme is followed and effective.

06

Pest control

Contracted pest programme, site map and inspection records. Either a contracted pest control service or a documented self-managed programme with appropriate records.

07

Hygiene policy, fitness to work procedure, training records and competency matrix. Everyone handling food must have appropriate training documented.

Recipe and finished product specifications, process monitoring records and calibration records. Demonstrates consistent production to specification.

Label approval procedure covering legal requirements and allergen declarations for all products. Labels must comply with the Food Information Regulations 2014.

Full trace from raw material to customer, batch coding and an annual traceability exercise. You must be able to trace any batch of product forward and backward within hours.

Recall procedure, incident response, complaint log and corrective action records. Evidence that when things go wrong, there is a procedure to follow and a record of what happened.

12

Version control, retention policy, food defence plan and cybersecurity policy. Documents must be controlled so that only current versions are in use and old versions are clearly identified.

How the audit works

What to expect on audit day.

A SALSA audit is conducted by a SALSA-approved auditor, typically an independent food safety professional approved by the SALSA organisation. The audit is announced, meaning you know it is coming, and typically lasts a full day for a small operation.

The auditor will review your documentation across all twelve compliance areas, inspect your premises and equipment, observe your processes in operation, and interview staff at various levels including production staff, not just management. The interview element catches businesses that have good documentation but whose teams do not actually follow or understand the procedures.

The audit produces a report with findings graded as major non-conformances, minor non-conformances and observations. Major non-conformances must be resolved before certification is granted. Minor non-conformances must be addressed within an agreed timeframe. Observations are recommendations rather than requirements.

Preparing for SALSA

The realistic timeline and what genuinely good preparation looks like.

For a business starting from scratch, three to six months is a realistic preparation timeline. The documentation can be produced faster, but the audit requires evidence that systems are actually being used. Temperature records need to have been completed. Cleaning schedules need to have been followed. Supplier approval procedures need to have been applied to real purchases. You cannot produce this evidence retrospectively.

HACCP explained
for startup food businesses.

HACCP is a legal requirement for every food business in the UK. This guide explains what it actually is, what it requires you to do, and how to build a HACCP plan that genuinely protects your business rather than just satisfying a checkbox.

What HACCP is

Hazard Analysis and Critical Control Points. In plain English.

HACCP is a systematic approach to identifying the things that could go wrong with food safety in your production process and putting controls in place to prevent them. It was originally developed for NASA's space food programme in the 1960s and has since become the global standard for food safety management.

The principle is straightforward. You analyse your production process step by step, identify every potential hazard at each step, whether biological, chemical or physical, assess the risk of each hazard occurring and its potential severity, and then determine whether a control measure at that step is critical to preventing the hazard from causing harm. Those critical steps become your Critical Control Points, and you put monitoring procedures in place to verify that the controls are working.

Regulation (EC) No 852/2004, retained in UK law after Brexit, makes HACCP a legal requirement for all food businesses. There is no minimum size threshold. Every food business must have a food safety management system based on HACCP principles.

The seven HACCP principles

The framework every HACCP plan is built on.

1

Conduct a hazard analysis

Identify every potential hazard associated with your food production process. Biological hazards include bacteria, viruses and parasites. Chemical hazards include cleaning agents, pesticides and allergens. Physical hazards include glass, metal, bone and packaging materials.

2

Identify critical control points

A critical control point is a step in your process where a control measure can be applied to prevent, eliminate or reduce a food safety hazard to an acceptable level. Common CCPs include cooking temperatures, chilling rates and metal detection.

3

Establish critical limits

For each CCP, define the measurable limit that separates safe from unsafe. For a cooking CCP this might be a minimum core temperature of 75 degrees Celsius for two minutes. The limit must be specific and measurable.

4

Establish monitoring procedures

Define how each CCP is monitored, how often, by whom, and using what equipment. Monitoring must be frequent enough to detect loss of control before it results in unsafe food.

5

Establish corrective actions

Define what happens when monitoring shows that a CCP is not under control. What do you do with the product? Who decides whether it is safe? How do you prevent the same failure recurring?

6

Establish verification procedures

Confirm that the HACCP system is working effectively. This includes reviewing monitoring records, checking calibration of monitoring equipment, conducting internal audits and reviewing the plan when anything changes.

7

Establish documentation and record keeping

Keep records demonstrating that the HACCP system is being followed. Monitoring records, corrective action records, verification records and the HACCP plan itself. Records are your due diligence defence if something goes wrong.

Common HACCP mistakes

Where food businesses get it wrong.

The most common mistake is treating HACCP as a document-writing exercise rather than a genuine analysis of your operation. A HACCP plan that was written to satisfy a requirement rather than to reflect reality will not protect your business during an inspection or audit, and will not protect consumers if something goes wrong.

Over-identifying critical control points is a frequent problem in self-written plans. If everything is a CCP, nothing is effectively controlled. A well-designed HACCP plan for a small producer typically has between two and six CCPs. If your plan has twenty, it probably needs rethinking.

Failing to review the plan when things change is the other major failure. A new product, a new supplier, a new piece of equipment, a change to your process. Each of these potentially changes your hazard analysis. A plan that was accurate on day one but has never been updated is a liability rather than a protection.

Environmental health inspections.
What to expect and how to be genuinely ready.

Environmental health officers inspect food businesses to protect public health and enforce food safety law. This guide covers how the inspection system works, what officers look for, what the food hygiene rating scheme means in practice, and what happens when things do not go well.

How the inspection system works

Who inspects you, when, and why.

Environmental health officers are employed by local authorities. They have the legal power under the Food Safety Act 1990 to enter food business premises at any reasonable time, inspect your operation, examine and take away food samples, and inspect your records. They do not need to make an appointment. The unannounced nature of inspections is deliberate. It means the business they see is the business you operate every day, not the business you put together for inspection day.

New food businesses are typically inspected within the first year of trading. After that, inspection frequency is risk-based. Businesses with a five rating and a good compliance history may not be inspected again for several years. Businesses with lower ratings or compliance issues are inspected more frequently. A complaint from a customer or another business can trigger an unplanned inspection at any time.

The food hygiene rating scheme

What the numbers actually mean.

The Food Hygiene Rating Scheme, operated by local authorities across England, Wales and Northern Ireland, rates food businesses from zero to five following an inspection. Scotland operates a similar scheme. The rating reflects the state of the business at the time of the inspection across three areas, which are weighted to produce the final score.

Food hygiene and safety (most heavily weighted)

How food is stored, handled, prepared and cooked. Temperature controls, cross-contamination prevention, personal hygiene. This area has the biggest impact on the final rating.

Structure, cleanliness and condition of the premises

The physical condition of the building, equipment and facilities. Damaged surfaces, inadequate ventilation, poorly maintained equipment and inadequate pest control all contribute to a lower score in this area.

Confidence in management systems

The food safety management system, documentation and record keeping. This is where HACCP plans, cleaning records, temperature logs, training records and allergen management documentation are assessed. Missing or inadequate documentation significantly reduces the rating in this area.

What happens after a poor rating

The practical and commercial consequences.

Food hygiene ratings are published on the Food Standards Agency website and must be displayed by food businesses in Wales and Northern Ireland. In England, display is currently encouraged but not mandatory for most business types, though many buyers and platforms require it regardless. The rating is publicly searchable and increasingly checked.

A rating below three creates immediate commercial problems. Buyers, retailers and food service operators who ask for your rating will see it. Online platforms that list food businesses may delist or restrict you. Some event organisers will not accept applications from businesses below a certain rating.

You can request a re-inspection after a minimum of three months. The local authority controls the timing and cannot guarantee when the re-inspection will happen. In the meantime, the original rating remains published. Businesses that improve their systems properly rather than making superficial changes before a re-inspection tend to achieve and sustain higher ratings.

Improvement notices issued during an inspection have legal force. They specify what must be done and by when. Failure to comply with an improvement notice can result in prosecution. Prohibition notices, issued in serious cases, can close a food business immediately.

The only reliable preparation

Operate correctly every day.

The businesses that consistently achieve and maintain a five rating are not the ones that prepare intensively for inspections. They are the ones that have built proper systems, embedded them into daily operations, and maintain them consistently. When an environmental health officer arrives unannounced, they see the same operation the business runs every day.

The businesses that struggle are the ones that either have not prioritised compliance or that have documentation in place but whose teams do not actually follow it. An officer who interviews staff and finds that nobody knows where the HACCP plan is, or what to do if a temperature check fails, will note that regardless of how impressive the file looks.

Food traceability requirements in the UK.
What the law requires and how to build a system that actually works.

Traceability is the ability to follow food through all stages of production, processing and distribution. It is a legal requirement for all food businesses, a core SALSA compliance area, and the first thing a buyer asks for when something goes wrong. This guide explains what is required and what good traceability looks like in practice.

The legal requirement

What UK food law requires from every food business.

Regulation (EC) No 178/2002, the General Food Law Regulation retained in UK law after Brexit, requires all food businesses to be able to identify from whom they have purchased food or food-producing animals, and to whom they have sold their products. This is the one-step-back, one-step-forward traceability principle.

In practice this means knowing who supplied every ingredient in every product you make, and being able to identify which customers received which batches of your products. If a food safety issue is identified with a specific raw material batch, you need to be able to identify which of your products it went into and which customers or retailers received those products, quickly enough to prevent harm.

The Food Safety Act 1990 due diligence defence reinforces this. If something goes wrong, you need records that demonstrate you took all reasonable precautions. Traceability records are a core part of that evidence.

What a traceability system covers

The components that make traceability work end to end.

Incoming goods records

Records of every delivery received. Supplier, product, quantity, batch or lot number, delivery date and any quality checks carried out on receipt. This is the start of your traceability chain. Without supplier batch records, you cannot trace backwards from your finished product to the raw materials it contains.

Production batch records

Records linking each batch of finished product to the ingredients used, the quantities used, the batch numbers of those ingredients, the date of production, the equipment used and the staff involved. This is the link between your incoming goods and your outgoing products.

Batch coding

Every batch of product must be clearly coded so that it can be linked to its production record. The code system needs to be consistent and unambiguous. A batch code on a product must be traceable to a specific production record within minutes, not hours.

Dispatch records

Records linking each batch of product to the customer or retailer it was dispatched to. Date of dispatch, quantity, customer details and the batch code of the product dispatched. This completes the forward traceability chain from raw material to end customer.

Annual traceability exercise

SALSA requires an annual exercise demonstrating that your traceability system works. Select a raw material batch and trace it forward through production to dispatch. Select a finished product batch and trace it backward to raw materials. Document the exercise and the time taken. The SALSA benchmark is to complete a full trace in four hours. Good digital systems make this a fifteen minute exercise.

Why most small producer traceability systems fail

The gaps that only become visible when you need them.

The most common traceability failure in small food businesses is not the absence of records but the absence of linkage. Businesses often have delivery notes, production records and dispatch records as separate documents that are not connected to each other. You can find the delivery note for a particular ingredient, and you can find the dispatch record for a particular customer, but you cannot quickly answer the question of whether that ingredient ended up in a product dispatched to that customer.

The second common failure is inconsistent batch coding. If the batch code on a finished product does not match the batch code in the production record, or if batch codes are used inconsistently across products, the traceability chain breaks at the point where it matters most.

A well-configured digital system solves both of these problems. Notion databases can link ingredient deliveries to production batches to dispatch records automatically, so the trace from raw material to customer is a database query rather than a manual search through folders and spreadsheets.
