About Michael

Twenty six years of finding out, the hard way,
how operations actually work.

No team of consultants. No junior staff doing the work while a senior partner takes the credit. When you work with Mucked*, you work with Michael. That is the point.

There is also a small Italian husband lurking about LinkedIn with the lofty title of Business Development Manager. He is very enthusiastic, extremely well connected and absolutely not allowed anywhere near client work. He handles the outreach. Michael handles everything else.

The early years

Michael learned how operations actually work the way most people who are genuinely good at it do, by being the person in the room when something went wrong, and the person who had to fix it.

He started in hotels at fourteen, working evenings and weekends. By seventeen, the owners of his local four star hotel were leaving him to run the place while they set up a different business. He went on to manage a hundred bedroom hotel with a high turnover of coach parties, where the only way to keep things running was to genuinely understand every part of the operation, not just the bit he'd been hired for.

The middle stretch

Kitchens in two countries, a hostel in the jungle, and a decade of festivals.

From there it was kitchens. Two years as head chef in Bordeaux, then a year doing the same in Paris, learning food safety and kitchen operations the way the French do it properly, under pressure, at pace, with no room for sloppiness. After that came a year in Italy, converting an old monastery into a working bed and breakfast from nothing, and a year managing a backpackers' hostel in the middle of the Guatemalan jungle that regularly ranked among the top ten in Central America, building the operational systems that let a business like that function with no infrastructure to rely on. Then Glasgow, as general manager of a high end restaurant with fifty staff, where the job was, again, making sure everything underneath the menu actually worked.

Running alongside most of this was a decade in festival operations, helping independent farmers, roasteries, cheesemakers, bakers and small food and drink producers get their products to market and get it right when they got there. That meant designing shared infrastructure so small producers could afford a pitch at a major festival without taking on the cost alone, building mobile, fully compliant prep facilities that meant a dozen traders could share one properly equipped, properly inspected space instead of each solving the same problem badly on their own, and working out the unglamorous logistics, queue flow, who sells next to who, how stock gets in and waste gets out, that decide whether a food festival actually runs or quietly falls apart. Over a decade, that added up to somewhere in the region of 150 to 200 events, including major national events and broadcast productions.

Forestry and agriculture

Sixty contractors, 500 hectares, and an app built to make it all work.

Alongside the festival work, Michael spent years self employed in agriculture and forestry, working directly with livestock from birth through to the table, and managing forestry operations involving chemical application, fencing and land management, working alongside councils, road authorities and the Ministry of Defence to keep everything compliant.

One job covered 500 hectares of hillside that needed chemically sprayed, using around 60 self employed contractors. Every one of them needed the right certificates on file, the right PPE checked before they were allowed up the hill, and a clear daily record of who was on site and who wasn't, on top of tracking chemical use for DEFRA and working out who'd sprayed what, since that's how they got paid. Pulling that together by hand, across 60 different people, was close to impossible. So Michael built an app for it. Contractors uploaded their certificates straight to it. A daily check in and check out showed exactly who was on the hill at any moment. A site supervisor had to physically sign off each contractor's PPE on the app before they were allowed to start work, no signature, no access to the hill. It tracked which areas had been sprayed, which fed straight into how everyone got paid, and gave the site manager the entire operation, certificates, attendance, safety sign offs, payment data, on their phone. What had been an administrative nightmare became something that ran itself.

Where it's led

Most recently, a startup distillery.

Most recently, that's meant working as second in command at a startup distillery, across everything from production compliance to the regulatory side of building a drinks business from the ground up.

Mucked* exists because of what every single one of those roles had in common. None of them were really about hospitality, or food, or farming, or drinks. They were about operations, the unglamorous, unphotographed work of making sure a business actually runs the way it's supposed to, whether that's a hotel, a hostel in the jungle, a festival site, a farm, or a distillery.

A small example of how that thinking works

£12,000, or £300. Same legal requirement, same sign-off.

A food business was setting up a new unit and needed a clear division between their production area and a small office space, a legal requirement under food safety regulations. The quotes coming in were for heavy duty industrial plastic curtain walling, ceiling to floor, at around £12,000. Michael had a look and suggested something different, large shelving units to block the space instead, with a gap left for a walkway, the shelving backed with branded, food safe panelling. Total cost, around £300. It was signed off by the environmental health officer without any issue. Same legal requirement met, same result on paper, a fraction of the price.

That's the instinct Blueprint, Engine Room and Whole Works are all built on. Find the version of "properly compliant" that actually works for the business in front of you, not the most expensive one.

"I've spent my career being the person who has to make things actually work, not just look right on paper. Compliance is the boring bit that needs to be done, because it's the thing every food and drink business needs and most haven't got quite right. After that, the fun begins."

The name

It is not an accident.

Mucked as in mucked in, mucked up, mucked about. It is British, warm, memorable and makes people do a double take for a split second before realising it is completely innocent. That moment is intentional. The name was chosen because it does not sound like a compliance consultancy. Which is exactly the point.
